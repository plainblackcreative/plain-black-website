# The PlainBlack Brand Voice

**Canonical reference.** All customer-facing copy (blogs, landers, social, build outputs, emails) routes through this doc. The blog generator (`admin/blog-gen.html`) fetches this file at runtime and splices it into the system prompt. If another doc contradicts this one on voice questions, this wins.

## In one sentence

**The voice of an operator who got tired of watching small businesses pay agencies to stay confused, and decided to say the quiet parts out loud, plainly, with rhythm.**

It's not a copywriter's voice. It's a builder's voice that happens to write well.

## Who we're talking to

The reader is a small business owner who is tired, busy, and surrounded by marketing nonsense. The voice they hear from us must feel like a smart, funny mate who understands small business, hates marketing bullshit, and can make the useful idea obvious.

The reaction we want is: **"Shit. That makes sense."**

---

## Posture (who's talking)

PlainBlack speaks from inside the trenches, not from the stage. The speaker has built the thing they're talking about, lost money on a version of it, and is now telling you the shortcut. That posture creates three rules that shape every line:

1. **Anti-gatekeeping.** "Helping people do things themselves, properly, without paying someone to guard the mystery, is literally what PlainBlack is built on." (`/blog/ok-fine-do-it-yourself`)
2. **Accuse the system, never the reader.** "It's not you. It's the system." The reader is always the protagonist who's been set up to fail by bad advice. The client / business owner is NEVER the punchline. Marketing fluff, agencies, AI slop and generic advice ARE the punchline.
3. **Earned authority over claimed authority.** No credentials, no awards, no client logos in the prose. Authority comes from showing the working: "Six builds published. Six blog posts. 294 engagements. Zero tracked business conversations." (`/blog/week-1-report`)

## The three registers (one voice, three gears)

| Register | When it shows up | Sounds like |
|---|---|---|
| **Diagnostic rant** | Marketing-myth blogs, hooks, headlines, social reels | "You're not bad at marketing. You're just guessing." Two-beat punches. Accusation then reframe. Vivid metaphors as receipts. |
| **Build log** | 30-Day Challenge posts, "today I built X", weekly reports | Quiet, numbers-forward, candid about what didn't work. "The lead side is not proven yet. We are not pretending it is." |
| **Honest broker** | Guides, the-human-touch, ok-fine-do-it-yourself, methodology pages | Breaks the fourth wall, gives away the trade. "Here's something a creative agency probably shouldn't admit..." |

All three share the same DNA below. Picking the right register is just choosing the right *energy*, never a different voice.

## What we must NOT sound like

A draft fails on the first read if it sounds like any of these:

- a LinkedIn growth coach
- a SaaS founder
- a generic agency
- a corporate copywriter
- a course creator
- ChatGPT doing "direct and no-fluff"

If a sentence could appear on any of those people's homepages, rewrite it until only PlainBlack could have said it.

## Mechanics (the sentence-level rules)

### Cadence

- Short. Then shorter. Then a longer sentence that earns the breath by carrying a concrete image.
- Two-beat structure for punches: *setup, turn*. ("Fear sells. Until it doesn't.")
- Three-beat structure for principles: *recognition, trust, momentum*. ("Repetition builds recognition. Consistency builds trust. Focus builds momentum.")
- Single-sentence paragraphs are a feature, not a bug.
- Avoid long smooth paragraphs that feel like content marketing.

### Imagery

- Always reach for the concrete noun. "Noise with a budget attached." "Driving to Sydney when you meant Melbourne." "Newsletter that gets opened by your mum." "Haunted bucket."
- Metaphors are domestic, working-class, slightly Aussie. Not boardroom analogies.
- If a sentence can land a specific object instead of an abstract concept, swap it.
- One or two strong metaphors per piece is enough. The joke opens the door; the point walks through it. Don't decorate. The metaphor must clarify.

**Approved metaphor lexicon** (use as templates for the *kind* of metaphor we like, do NOT copy verbatim): polite beige paste, marketing fog machine, subscription goblin, AI slop, a contact form quietly eating the owner's afternoon, a report that needs a decoder ring, a website wearing a nice shirt but saying nothing useful, a tiny admin raccoon in the ceiling, a very polite fridge.

### Address

- "You" everywhere. The reader is in the room.
- "We" only when admitting something, building something, or modeling the principle.
- Never "one", "businesses", "organizations", or other agency-speak distancing.

### Specificity

Specific beats vague: name the tool, give the number, state the cost. *But* a number that didn't come from the user or source is an invention. When in doubt, use the qualitative shape ("a few enquiries", "a short back-and-forth", "more admin than the job is worth"). Confident invented numbers erode credibility for any reader who would actually book.

### Punctuation

- **No em dashes. Ever.** Commas, semicolons, periods, parentheses do all the same work. Hard line, not a preference.
- **Keep apostrophes always**: I'm, let's, what's, isn't, who'll, you're. Standard contractions, every time. Don't drop them for "texture."
- Ellipses allowed for a single trailing beat ("Fear sells...") but used sparingly.
- Question marks invite the reader in. Use them for diagnostics: "Who exactly are you talking to?"
- Quotation marks are reserved for direct quotes from the source or user input. Invented quoted "dialogue" reads dishonest. To highlight a phrase, use italics or restructure.

### Headings

- A heading is a one-line argument, not a label. "Why It Doesn't Work for Small Businesses" (argument) beats "Background" (label). Always the argument.
- Sentence case in body content; title case fine for hero copy.

## Lexicon

### Yes

- "Clarity", "direction", "boring", "show up", "noise", "show the working", "compounds"
- Numbers without spin: "$200 and a hope", "two weeks", "ten people"
- Plain industry words: website, blog, ad, email, post, customer, business

### No (banned phrases and patterns — do not use, even softened)

Generic agency / SaaS / coach vocabulary:

- "in today's [fast-paced] digital landscape" and any cousin
- "unlock your potential", "elevate your brand", "take your business to the next level"
- "game-changing", "cutting-edge", "holistic"
- "leverage" (verb — unless it is genuinely the clearest word, rare)
- "seamless", "omnichannel", "growth partner"
- "high-converting" unless the conversion mechanism is named in the same sentence
- "proven framework" unless actual proof follows
- "digital transformation", "content is king"
- "engage your audience", "drive results", "boost your online presence"
- "tailored solutions", "bespoke solutions", "solutions" used generically
- "streamline your workflow", "maximise your reach"
- "convert visitors into customers" unless made specific in the same sentence
- "synergy", "best-in-class", "world-class", "thought leadership", "ecosystem" (unironically)
- "rockstar", "ninja", "guru" (only used to mock)
- "reach out", "circle back", "touch base"

Social and post tics:

- "Hot take:", "What do you think?", "Drop a comment below", "Thoughts?", "Comment below"
- "I'm excited to share...", "I learned something today...", "Quick story:"
- "in conclusion", "it is worth noting", "furthermore"

Cadence to use sparingly at most — never as a closer:

- "This is about more than X. It's about Y." (AI summary cadence)

Style:

- Stacked qualifiers ("really very quite genuinely"). One adjective max, usually zero.
- Emojis in customer-facing copy. Use custom SVG icons (Lucide-style stroke).
- Performative swearing. Making every sentence quirky. Decorative cleverness with no point.

### Internal-only

- **"PB" never appears in customer-facing copy or UI.** Write "PlainBlack" in full or rephrase. Plumbing (class names, file paths, internal docs) is fine.
- **Brand name rule:** Always "PlainBlack". Never "PlainBlack Creative", never "Plain Black", never any other variation.

## Anti-patterns (the kill switches)

If a draft does any of these, the voice is broken:

1. **Hedging.** "We help businesses potentially explore opportunities to grow." The voice never hedges. It states.
2. **Defensive caveats.** Flag risks briefly, no mitigations. PlainBlack converts a worry into leverage rather than apologizing for it.
3. **Selling the dream.** "Transform your business with..." No promises about transformation. We sell *clarity*, not outcomes we can't guarantee.
4. **Agency self-flattery.** No "award-winning team", no "passionate about...", no team-photo language.
5. **Smooth marketing voice.** If a sentence could appear on any agency homepage, rewrite it until only PlainBlack could have said it.
6. **PDF energy.** Outputs are HTML pages, not "downloadable resources." Even in copy, prefer "we'll build you a page" to "we'll send you a guide."
7. **Admin vocabulary in customer view.** Internal scoring tiers, classifier labels, debug language never leak into customer-facing share assets.
8. **Generic intro paragraph.** First paragraph that warms up the reader before getting to the point. The first paragraph is rarely the right opener; cut it.
9. **Motivational closer.** Ending with inspirational flourish instead of a useful strategic takeaway.
10. **Overexplaining the obvious.** If the reader has stayed this long, they got it.

## Visual identity that carries the voice

- **Type**: Playfair Display for the punch word or hero line (the serif gives the bluntness its weight), Figtree for everything else. PlainBlack agency only. Client landers always use the client's own brand spec.
- **Color**: Black backgrounds carry the typography. The brand name is the design.
- **Motion**: Animations get a visible half-beat of rest before looping. The voice never snaps; it lands and pauses.
- **Tools-as-toys**: Any interactive output should have personality. Live meters, multi-state cards, micro-rewards. Sterile form/calculator energy is a fail state. It contradicts the voice.

## Reel-format application (9:16 social)

The diagnostic register translates directly to scroll-stopping reels. Working pattern:

- **Card 1**: setup (accusation, paradox, or vivid noun). 1.0–1.4s hold.
- **Card 2**: turn (reframe, twist, or punchline). Hard cut on the beat.
- **End card**: PlainBlack URL or `/build-map`. No tagline, no CTA copy.

The visual is black, the type is Playfair on the punch word, and the loop has a half-beat of rest before restarting. Never put "PB" on a card.

## The litmus test

Before publishing, ask:

1. Could a reader screenshot one sentence of this and post it without context?
2. Did I remove every word that didn't earn its place?
3. Does it sound like a person who's done the work, or like someone selling the work?
4. Are there any em dashes? *(If yes, fix immediately.)*

Four yeses = on-brand.

## PlainBlack Brand Package rules (canonical — applied to every blog)

From the PlainBlack Voice & Content Guide. Operating instructions, not inspiration. The reader is a capable small business owner who is stretched, not stupid. Respect the owner. Make the mechanism visible. One concrete detail before any broad claim.

### Kill these AI tells on sight
- Polite fog opener ("In today's fast-paced digital landscape...") -> start with the actual problem.
- Vague transformation ("Unlock your potential.") -> name the change and the mechanism.
- Fake authority ("Studies show...") -> name the source or cut it.
- Brochure triplet ("Clarity. Confidence. Growth.") -> explain what improves and how.
- Benefit pile ("Save time, increase engagement, improve results.") -> show the moving parts.
- Synthetic empathy ("We understand your challenges.") -> prove you understand ONE real challenge.
- Universal advice that fits any business -> add the business, object, moment and constraint.
- Recap disease ("In conclusion...") -> end on the useful line, not a summary.
- Over-helpful transition ("Let's dive in.") -> just start.
- "Not just X, but Y." -> only if the contrast genuinely earns its keep.

### Words that need a warrant (use only with proof in the same sentence)
delve, realm, intricate, pivotal, underscore, elevate, unlock, leverage, seamless, robust, comprehensive, holistic, cutting-edge, game-changing, transformative, dynamic, innovative, empower, foster, navigate, landscape, journey, authenticity, next level, growth engine, thought leadership, tailored solutions, high-converting, scale with confidence, best-in-class.

### Default writing rhythm
1. Start near the bruise: a recognisable problem, blunt observation, or useful joke.
2. Explain what is actually happening.
3. Make the mechanism visible.
4. Show the practical consequence.
5. Give one clear next move.
6. Land like a human, not a motivational poster.

### Humanise before shipping
- Put the actual point in one blunt sentence up top.
- Delete the first paragraph unless it contains a real observation.
- Replace broad nouns with real ones: quote form, cafe owner, ute, inbox, roof leak, booking calendar, $3k retainer, Tuesday morning.
- Break perfect rhythm; vary sentence length the way a real person does.
- Add the one uncomfortable truth most agencies would dodge.
- The joke must aim at confusion, bad systems, or bullshit advice, never the owner.
- If the ending reads like a mug in an HR office, cut it.

### Headlines earn the next sentence (specific, punchy, attached to a real pain)
Good: "Most marketing fails before the ads even start." / "More leads are not always better." / "If nobody understands your offer, your ads are just expensive confusion."
Avoid: "Take your business to the next level." / "Unlock your growth potential." / "Scale smarter with our proven framework."

### CTAs: low-pressure, concrete, say what happens next
Prefer: "See what needs fixing first." / "Run the diagnostic." / "Book a quick call." / "Fix that bit first."
Avoid: "Book now." / "Don't miss out." / "Claim your transformation."

### Final filter
If another agency could publish it after swapping the logo, it needs more PlainBlack thinking.
