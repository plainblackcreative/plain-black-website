# PlainBlack Visual Brand Guide

**Claude Code operating document**  
**Status:** Canonical working guide  
**Use for:** website design, landing pages, social graphics, blog heroes, OG images, pitch decks, UI concepts, image-generation prompts, tool pages, client-facing brand boards and presentation systems.
## 1. Core visual position

PlainBlack visuals should not look like a normal marketing agency with a dark theme.

They should feel like a strategic small-business invention studio:

**dark cinematic strategy · practical intelligence · small-business grit · mint-green clarity cutting through the mess**

The visual job is simple:

> Make PlainBlack look like the place where messy business problems become clear, useful and actionable.

Not cute. Not corporate. Not beige. Not an AI startup with a gradient and a dream.

## 3. Visual personality

PlainBlack should look like:

- a late-night strategy room;
- a workshop bench with a marketing plan on it;
- a detective board for business clarity;
- a black notebook full of useful ideas;
- a small-business owner finally seeing the next move;
- a cinematic still from a film about fixing what everyone else made complicated;
- a field manual for owners who are sick of being sold fog;
- premium dark stationery, tools, screens, notes and proof objects;
- Australia/New Zealand practical grit with a sharper creative edge.

PlainBlack should not look like:

- generic SaaS branding;
- bright startup gradients;
- smiling stock-photo teams high-fiving near a laptop;
- sterile agency case-study templates;
- fake futuristic AI dashboards;
- shiny corporate boardrooms;
- over-polished motivational posters;
- Canva-template marketing graphics;
- personal-brand guru assets;
- American webinar funnel visuals;
- empty dark-mode tech aesthetics with no commercial idea.

If another agency could use the design after swapping the logo, it is too generic.

### Supporting environment colours

Use only as environmental support:

- warm tungsten amber;
- aged-paper beige;
- worn metal;
- coffee brown;
- charcoal concrete;
- deep bottle-green shadow;
- soft screen glow.

### Ratio rule

A strong PlainBlack visual usually uses:

- **75–85%** black, charcoal and shadow;
- **10–20%** warm practical environment;
- **3–7%** mint-green signal;
- white reserved for wordmark and key text.

Mint is not decoration. It is the clue, system, action point, proof object or next move.

Avoid bright corporate blue, purple SaaS gradients, soft wellness pastels, flat green backgrounds, rainbow neon and red unless it is deliberately signalling danger or a broken system.
## 5. Logo rules

Use the full wordmark when the asset needs clear external ownership: covers, headers, heroes, slides, templates and client-facing artefacts.

Preferred placements:

- top left;
- lower left;
- centre-left on a clean dark field;
- subtly in-world on a notebook, mug, card, sign, screen or wall.

Keep at least one logomark-height of breathing room around the wordmark.

Do not:

- stretch, squash or rotate the wordmark;
- recolour it randomly;
- add drop shadows to rescue bad contrast;
- place it over busy texture without a dark overlay;
- rebuild it in another font;
- place it on the villain, snake-oil bottle, broken object or system being criticised.

PlainBlack is the observer and useful signal. It is not the problem.

### Practical wordmark sizing defaults

|---|---:|---:|

| 1600px hero image | `160px` wide | `200–280px` wide |
| Presentation slide | `0.9in` wide | `1.1–1.6in` wide |
| Website header | — | `140–190px` wide desktop; `120–160px` mobile |

Use the supplied logo assets directly. Never ask an image generator to recreate the wordmark.
## 6. Logomark rules

The mint circular logomark is the signal object. It can act as:

- a small stamp;
- phone-screen icon;
- notebook emboss;
- sticker;
- lapel pin;
- mug detail;
- glowing sign;
- projected light;
- desk token;
- stencil;
- subtle pattern;
- hidden easter egg.

It should feel like signal, clarity, fingerprint, proof, focus or a useful clue.

### Easter egg rule

Use hidden details as a second-look reward:

- simple social tile: 1–2;
- blog hero: 2–4;
- complex cinematic scene: 3–5;
- logo-led identity asset: none required.

Good placements: notebook corner, laptop sticker, mug, phone screen, tape measure, card, chalk mark, tool chest, shop reflection, book spine or small sign.

Bad placements: villain shirt, scam product, broken object, repeated wallpaper or anything that implies PlainBlack caused the problem.

The first read is the idea. The second read rewards attention.

## 7. Typography system

PlainBlack typography should create contrast between authority, clarity and human practicality. Use the following stacks and default sizes before inventing anything. Break the defaults only when there is a clear design reason.

### A. Display serif — for campaign moments

Use for hero headlines, big statements, section covers, manifesto lines, brand-led social graphics and high-impact presentation slides.

Preferred fonts:

1. **Cormorant Garamond**
2. **Playfair Display**
3. **Libre Baskerville**
4. **Georgia** fallback

Feeling: premium, editorial, sharp, timeless and slightly dramatic.

Do not use display serif for dense body copy, UI labels, tool screens, tiny captions or practical instructions.

### B. Practical sans — for clarity and instruction

Use for body text, slide copy, UI cards, labels, captions, proposals, landing pages, supporting lines and anything that needs to be read quickly by a tired small business owner.

Preferred fonts:

1. **Figtree**
2. **Inter**
3. **Aptos**
4. **Helvetica Neue** fallback
5. **Arial** emergency fallback, but only if the machines have started fighting back

Feeling: clean, practical, readable, modern, calm and not overly techy.

### C. Mono/system font — for tool labels and evidence

Use sparingly for diagnostics, system cards, annotations, file labels, prompt blocks, audit stamps, code-like elements, status tags and “this is the useful machine bit” moments.

Preferred fonts:

1. **IBM Plex Mono**
2. **Space Mono**
3. **Roboto Mono**
4. **Courier New** fallback

Feeling: evidence, field notes and diagnostic clarity.

### D. Handwritten or marker text — for in-scene human marks only

Use only inside scenes: notebooks, whiteboards, sticky notes, chalkboards, rough workshop plans, circled insights and margin notes.

Use rough but legible marker text. White or mint on dark. Black ink on paper. Avoid fake serial-killer webinar handwriting. Claude will try. Do not let it.

### Master hierarchy

PlainBlack layouts should usually use three text layers only:

1. **Dominant headline** — the idea.
2. **Support line** — the useful explanation.
3. **Small labels** — evidence, tags, notes and UI details.

Do not turn the asset into a ransom note of competing messages.

A strong layout usually follows this scale relationship:

- hero headline: `100%`;
- supporting line: `35–48%` of headline size;
- small labels: `18–26%` of headline size;
- footer or microcopy: `14–18%` of headline size.

Example: headline `96px`; support `38–44px`; label `18–22px`; footer `14–16px`.

## 8. Spacing system

Use an 8px spacing system for digital design.

Default spacing tokens:
- **4px** micro gap
- **8px** tight gap
- **16px** close grouping
- **24px** standard grouping
- **32px** section breathing
- **48px** major separation
- **64px** premium separation
- **96px** hero breathing
- **128px** large web section spacing

### Social spacing
- Space between label and headline: **18–28px**
- Space between headline and support: **24–40px**
- Space between support and CTA/footer: **36–60px**
- Logo clear space: at least **1× logomark height**

### Slide spacing
- Space above headline: use margin, not random nudging.
- Headline to body: **18–30pt**
- Body block to next object: **22–36pt**
- Card inner padding: **18–28pt**
- Footer from bottom edge: **0.22–0.35 in**

### Web spacing
- Hero top/bottom padding: **96–156px desktop**, **64–96px mobile**
- Section padding: **80–128px desktop**, **56–80px mobile**
- Card gap: **24–36px**
- Form field gap: **14–18px**

## 9. Image style

PlainBlack imagery is usually:

- dark cinematic;
- high contrast;
- practical and grounded;
- textured;
- slightly gritty;
- premium but lived-in;
- composed like a film still;
- filled with meaningful objects;
- rich in detail without visual mud.

### Lighting

Prefer:

- low-key cinematic lighting;
- directional desk-lamp pools;
- warm tungsten practical lights;
- soft screen glow;
- restrained mint rim light or glow;
- dramatic shadow;
- light haze or dust when it helps.

Avoid flat bright lighting, sterile studios, influencer lighting, blue-purple cyberpunk soup and scenes so dark the useful idea disappears.

### Texture

Use matte black surfaces, worn wood, concrete, metal, paper, notebooks, tools, fingerprints, film grain, subtle scratches and coffee rings. Real, not filthy.

### Composition

Use a strong hero object, clear negative space, depth of field, a visual path toward the mint signal and supporting details that reward inspection.

Do not blur everything into moody soup.

## 11. Asset archetypes

### A. Strategy desk

A dark desk or bench with notebook, laptop, mug, pen and practical plan. Use for playbooks, diagnostics, services, frameworks and tool pages.

### B. Workshop reality

A gritty but respectful small-business environment: mechanic workshop, trade bench, cafe back room, local service office. PlainBlack enters through a plan, screen, note, checklist or mint signal.

### C. Detective board / clarity wall

Use when the message is diagnosis, prioritisation or making sense of a mess. Mint highlights the useful path.

### D. Anti-agency theatre

A cinematic satire of fake scarcity, bloated webinars, snake oil, confusing reports, jargon and value-stack nonsense. The critique should be obvious. PlainBlack branding stays at the edge as observer and alternative.

### E. Tool product hero

A polished visual for a playbook, diagnostic, translator, engine, public utility or proof object. Show the outcome. Do not make it look like a cheap AI wrapper.

### F. Founder / character cinematic

Dark background, human expression, mint rim light, no influencer worship. Slightly odd. Grounded. Useful.

### G. Premium identity system

Logo-led assets, black notebooks, embossed marks, stationery, mockups and strong negative space. Minimal clutter.
## 12. Layout rules

Use this hierarchy:

1. brand cue;
2. main headline;
3. supporting line;
4. visual proof or object;
5. CTA or footer detail, only if needed.

Keep important content inside the central 80% to allow platform cropping. Avoid tiny text near the bottom edge.

Common ratios:

| Ratio | Use |
|---|---|
| `16:9` | blog hero, website hero, YouTube, LinkedIn banners, presentation covers. |
| `4:5` | Facebook, Instagram and LinkedIn feed graphics. |
| `1:1` | Simple social tiles and profile graphics. |
| `9:16` | Reels, stories and mobile concepts. |

Text in images should be short. All AI-generated text must be checked manually.

Good lines:

- Clear advice. Real results. No guesswork.
- Stop guessing what to fix first.
- Less guesswork. More yes.
- Strategy before design.
- Built for business, not just browsers.
- Make it useful. Make it matter.
- Marketing should make sense before it costs more money.

- The useful bits, circled.
- Your next move, made obvious.

Avoid:

- Unlock your potential.
- Take your brand to the next level.
- Growth made simple.
- Transform your business.

- Empower your digital journey.

## 14. Presentation and pitch rules

PlainBlack presentations should feel strategic, calm and premium without becoming corporate theatre.

Use:

- large dark slides;
- strong serif headings;
- simple sans-serif support copy;
- one idea per slide;
- generous spacing;
- restrained mint highlights;
- practical mockups;
- before/after examples;
- strategic labels;
- visual systems, not random options.

Avoid dense text, colourful templates, buzzword diagrams, filler stock photography and “logo option 1 / 2 / 3” framing.

For identity presentations, ask:

> Which future for this business feels most true?

Not:

> Which logo do you like?
## 15. Website design rules

A PlainBlack website should feel like a digital home, not a business card wearing a form at the bottom.

It should:

- make the offer understandable quickly;
- show the thinking behind the thing;
- help people choose the right next step;
- filter bad-fit enquiries;
- give proof without corporate proof theatre;
- show useful tools and artefacts;
- use restrained motion;
- keep readability ahead of mood;
- use mint to guide action;
- feel custom, not templated.
## 16. Claude visual production rules

When Claude creates a PlainBlack visual, it must specify:

1. Canvas size and aspect ratio.
2. Font family for each text level.
3. Exact text sizes.
4. Line height.
5. Safe margins.
6. Colour palette with hex values.
7. Logo placement and size.

Claude must not say “use bold typography” without naming the size, weight, and hierarchy. That is how template sludge gets through the side door wearing a lanyard.

## 17. AI image and layout prompt formula

Build prompts in this order:

1. asset purpose;
2. specific small-business pain or visual metaphor;
3. practical hero object;
4. environment;
5. lighting and texture;
6. restrained mint signal;
7. meaningful artefacts;
8. 1–5 easter eggs depending on complexity;
9. negative space for copy if needed;
10. explicit avoid list.

### Reusable prompt base

```text
Create a dark cinematic PlainBlack-style visual for [asset purpose]. Show [specific small-business situation or visual metaphor] and communicate [clear commercial idea]. Make it feel premium, practical, gritty and useful, with deep black shadows, warm practical lighting, subtle film grain, textured surfaces and restrained mint-green accents. Include real-world business artefacts such as notebooks, checklists, tools, laptops, signs, cards, mugs or whiteboards. Add 2–4 subtle PlainBlack easter eggs using the mint circular logomark around the edges of the scene, but do not make them the main subject. Keep the business owner capable and respected. Aim any humour or critique at confusing marketing systems, not the client. Leave clean negative space for headline and logo if required. Avoid generic corporate stock photography, SaaS gradients, fake futuristic holograms, overdone neon, smiling boardroom people, unreadable AI text, distorted logos and anything that makes PlainBlack look like the problem being criticised.
```

Use this when asking Claude to create PlainBlack visuals.


## 19. Visual QA checklist

Before approval, ask:

1. Is the commercial idea clear in three seconds?
2. Does this feel unmistakably PlainBlack?
3. Is mint guiding attention, not decorating?
4. Is the logo used accurately and respectfully?
5. Are hidden marks placed where PlainBlack is the clue, not the villain?
6. Is the image cinematic without becoming muddy?
7. Does it avoid generic agency and SaaS cliches?
8. Is the owner capable and respected?
9. Is there negative space for crop and copy?
10. Is the typography readable?
11. Has AI-generated text been checked manually?
12. Could another agency use this after changing the logo?
13. Is there a useful idea beneath the polish?
14. Would a tired small business owner understand why it matters?

If number 12 is yes, start again. The design is probably wearing someone else's jacket.

Also check that the font sizes remain readable at actual platform size, the hierarchy has enough breathing room, and the design would still feel PlainBlack without the logo. Fix the thinking before polishing the pixels.
## 20. Final principle

PlainBlack visuals should not merely look cool. They should make the viewer feel:

> Someone finally understands the mess I am in, and they can show me what actually matters.

The rest is lighting, ink, mint and not letting an image generator invent another logo.
