30-Day Challenge

Our blogs are good. Our audience doesn’t read. So we fixed that.

4 min read By Jayden Brown 29 May 2026
← Back to Blog

We know our audience. Small business owners. Trades. Operators. People who are doing the job and running the business at the same time. People who are not sitting down to read a 1,200-word article over a quiet coffee.

They are in the ute. They are between jobs. They are driving to a site meeting. They are doing the dishes and half-listening to something in the background.

They listen. They do not read.

We have known this for a while. The blogs are good. The format is wrong for a chunk of the people we are trying to reach. Day 29 was the fix.

What we built

Not a podcast. Not a separate channel. Not a new RSS feed to maintain or a Spotify show to manage or a production workflow to set up.

Just the blog post, made listenable, on the same page it always lived.

Each post now has a small audio player above the article. A dark strip with a mint play button, a progress bar, and a speed toggle. Ian reads the post. You listen if you want. If you prefer to read, nothing has changed. Same URL, same content, same page.

The same post. Same URL. Just listenable now, for the people who were never going to read it.

How it works

Ian records each post using ElevenLabs with his cloned voice. We prepare the text with conversational pacing markers before he records it: ellipses where he would naturally pause, spaced dashes between sections where the reader would see a heading, short sentences broken onto their own lines so each one lands before the next begins.

The result is a reading that sounds like Ian talking, not Ian dictating. Punchy where the copy is punchy. Measured where the argument is building. It drops properly at the end instead of trailing off like there is another sentence coming.

The audio file drops into a folder in the repo. The player is wired into the blog HTML. No third-party player, no external embed, no cookies or tracking beyond our own GA event when someone hits play.

What we learned doing the first few

ElevenLabs has a 5,000 character limit per generation. Longer posts need to be split into two parts and stitched together. That is a five-minute job with the right tool, but it adds a step.

The Studio mode in ElevenLabs gives you more control over individual sentences, but it changes how some lines land. For Ian’s voice, the standard generation with well-prepared pacing text produces a more consistent result than manually overriding sentence by sentence.

The most important discovery: the ending. TTS engines default to reading the final sentence like there is more coming. The fix is to isolate the last three or four lines onto their own short paragraphs, so the engine registers each full stop as a complete thought rather than a continuation. Once you hear the difference, you cannot unhear it.

What is coming

We are rolling this out across the blog. Not every post, but the ones that are doing real work: the posts people find through search, the ones that get shared, the ones that make a real argument. Those get audio versions.

The player code is the same block every time. Adding audio to a post is a ten-minute job once the recording is done. The bottleneck is Ian’s recording time, not the build work.

If the listenable posts perform differently to the reading-only versions, we will write about it. That is the kind of thing worth knowing.
