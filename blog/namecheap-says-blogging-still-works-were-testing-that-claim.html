Namecheap says blogging still works. We're testing whether they're right.

6 min read By PlainBlack 10 May 2026
← Back to Blog

Namecheap published an article on 19 April titled "5 Reasons Blogging Is Still Relevant". The piece argues that blogging hasn't died despite AI, algorithm shifts, and the general sense that nobody reads anything anymore. They list stats, format advantages, personality, evolution, and niches as their five pillars.

We're nine days into a 30-day challenge that's effectively a live test of their thesis. Build-in-public posts every day, SEO-heavy, 100% organic, zero ad spend. If blogs still work for small businesses in 2026, we'll see traffic. If they don't, we'll see silence.

Here's where Namecheap is right, where they're vague, and what we're actually learning so far.

Point 1: The stats still speak for themselves

Namecheap cites 70 million new posts per month on WordPress alone, growing year on year. Their takeaway: "It's a big pie, and it's your job to find a way to carve out a teensy slice."

Scale alone is meaningless. 70 million new posts a month means you're not finding any audience by being one of the 70 million. You find audience by being the right one of the niche slice you're going for. The stat tells you the channel is busy, not that it works.

What matters: did someone in your niche search the question you answered, find your post, and read it? That's the only stat that pays the rent.

Point 2: The blog format hits a sweet spot

They argue blogs sit between informative and informal, skimmable but substantive. They acknowledge the AI summary problem: won't AI just steal your content and not direct anyone to your blog? Their answer: "It boils down to trust. AI cannot replicate first-hand experiences."

That answer is too neat. Yes, AI summaries reduce clicks. Yes, trust matters. But we're testing whether trust transfers to a small business with no existing audience. If you've never heard of us and Google's AI overview gives you the gist of what we wrote, why click through?

The bet: if the post has personality, if it argues with you or makes you laugh or annoys you enough to keep reading, you click. If it's just informative, the AI summary ate your lunch.

We're writing accordingly. Every post has a position. If you can lift a paragraph into ChatGPT and it blends in, we rewrote it.

Point 3: Personality is the thing AI can't fake

This is the strongest point in the article and the one we're betting on. Namecheap writes: "The unavoidable, tangible personality embedded within them is what sets blogs apart. Writers are imperfect, and blogs are the perfect medium to expose that."

AI slop has zero personality. It hedges every claim, balances every sentence, and sounds like a committee wrote it. Posts that argue with you, admit failure, or call out something specific have all of it.

If your blog reads like it could have been written by anyone in your industry, it's not doing the one thing blogs still do better than any other format.

We're nine days in. The posts getting traction are the ones with teeth. The ones that just explain a process got indexed and ignored.

Point 4: Constant evolution

Namecheap says blogs have evolved for 25 years, from diaries to marketing tools, and they'll keep evolving. Vague. Evolving how, for whom?

We're also evolving, but our evolution is "ship it and watch the numbers". We built a blog generator that writes in our voice because writing a post every day by hand would have killed the challenge by day five. That generator is the evolution. By the way, this very post was written using that tool.

The article name-drops high-level bloggers earning millions in 2026. True, but those are outliers with decade-old audiences. If you're starting now, you're not racing them. You're racing the clock on whether organic content brings in a single qualified lead before you give up.

Point 5: The infinite nature of niches

Namecheap writes: "If you can demonstrate passion, originality, and an untapped angle, an audience will follow."

This is the one we're literally testing right now. We're a niche audience: small business owners in AU/NZ/US who have been burned by marketing fluff and want a branding system that doesn't require a retainer. Either they show up or they don't.

The infinite-niches argument assumes the niche is findable. That's the open question. Can a new blog with no backlinks, no domain authority, and no existing audience rank for anything useful in 2026? Or does Google's algo favour age and authority so heavily that organic is dead for new entrants?

We'll know in 21 days. If organic flops, the next 30-day challenge is "30 days of ad spend and a holiday". Half joking, half not.

What we're learning so far

Nine days in, here's what's true:

  • Posts with personality get more time on page. Posts that just explain get bounces.
  • SEO still matters. If the post doesn't answer a search query, nobody finds it.
  • AI summaries do reduce clicks, but not to zero. If the summary is vague or incomplete, people click through.
  • Writing every day is brutal. The blog generator keeps it survivable.

Namecheap's article ends with a resounding yes, blogs are still relevant. We don't know yet. We'll have data at day 30. If you're reading this, you're part of the test.
