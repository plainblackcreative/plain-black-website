Namecheap Says Blogging Still Works. We're Testing That Claim.

4 min read By PlainBlack 9 May 2026
← Back to Blog

Namecheap published an article titled "5 Reasons Blogging Is Still Relevant." The page threw a 403 error when we tried to scrape it, which is either ironic or perfectly on-brand for a post about content marketing in 2026.

We're nine days into a 30-day experiment to find out for ourselves whether blogs still pull weight. One hundred percent organic, SEO-heavy, build-in-public posts every day. Zero ad spend. This is day nine.

We can't quote Namecheap's five reasons because we couldn't access the article, but the headline alone is enough to work with. The question isn't whether blogging is theoretically relevant. The question is whether it works for your business, in your market, with your resources.

The honest position

We don't know yet if blogs work for our market. We'll know in 21 days.

If organic flops, the next 30-day challenge is "30 days of ad spend and a holiday." That's a joke, but only half.

Most blogging advice treats SEO like a solved problem. It isn't. Google rewrote the rules three times in the last 18 months, Reddit now outranks most small business blogs, and AI overviews are eating the traffic that used to go to your top-ranking posts.

What we're actually testing

This experiment has three questions:

  • Can a small business blog rank for commercial intent keywords in 2026 without paying for backlinks or running ads?
  • If it ranks, does the traffic convert, or is it just browsers?
  • Is the time investment worth it compared to paid channels?

We're publishing the results whether they're good or catastrophic. If blogging works, we'll show you the numbers. If it doesn't, we'll show you those too, and we'll move on to the next test.

Why most blogging advice is useless

Generic blogging advice assumes you have unlimited time, a content team, and a market that still uses Google the way they did in 2019. Most small businesses have none of those things.

The standard playbook is: write 2,000-word posts, target long-tail keywords, build backlinks, wait six months. That might work if you're selling SaaS to enterprise buyers. It probably doesn't work if you're a tradie trying to pull local leads in a city with 47 competitors.

We're not saying blogging is dead. We're saying the question "does blogging work?" is too broad to be useful. The real question is: does blogging work for you, in your market, with your time budget?

What we know so far

Nine days in, here's what we've learned:

  • Publishing daily is harder than it looks on paper. The discipline matters more than the tool.
  • Most of the friction is in the setup, not the writing. Once the system is built, the publishing loop gets faster.
  • Traffic is still negligible. SEO takes time. Anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something.

The real test starts in week three, when Google has had time to index the posts and we can see whether any of them are pulling organic traffic.

The PlainBlack take

Don't start a blog because an article told you to. Start a blog because you have a hypothesis you want to test, and you're willing to publish the results whether they prove you right or wrong.

We'll report back on day 30. If blogging works, we'll show you the setup. If it doesn't, we'll show you what we're testing next.
