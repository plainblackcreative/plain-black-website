Namecheap says blogging still works. We're testing whether they're right.

5 min read By PlainBlack 10 May 2026
← Back to Blog

Ten days into a 30-day challenge to find out if blogging still works for a small business in 2026. Daily posts, SEO-heavy, build-in-public, zero ad spend. We do not know yet if it works. We will know in 20 days.

This week, Namecheap published "5 Reasons Blogging Is Still Relevant"; a sponsored piece for their Bloggers' Week, which is a hosting-company sale. They sell hosting, of course they want you to start a blog. Their answer is "yes." Our answer, while we are literally running the experiment, is "we don't know yet." Both can be right; they are talking theory, we are talking practice.

Their five reasons are reasonable on paper. Let's go through them.

1. The stats still speak for themselves

Their take: up to 70 million new posts a month on WordPress alone, growing year on year. "It's a big pie, and it's your job to find a way to carve out a teensy slice."

Where they are right: yes, the medium is enormous and growing.

Where they are wrong: scale alone is meaningless. 70 million new posts a month means you are not finding any audience by being one of the 70 million. Big-pie thinking dies the moment you realise the pie is so big nobody can see your slice. You don't win by joining the pile, you win by being the right one for the niche slice you are going for. Their own point five (niches) actually makes their first point look weaker.

2. The blog format

Their take: skimmable, the sweet spot between informative and informal, fun list-style content. Plus a fair acknowledgement of the AI-summaries-eat-clicks problem, with their answer being "uncited AI answers get followed by users asking for sources" and "it boils down to trust." AI cannot replicate first-hand experiences.

Where they are right: the format genuinely is unique. Not as throwaway as a magazine, not as dry as Wikipedia. Plenty of room for personality.

Where their answer is too neat: "boils down to trust" works in theory and skips the actual question, which is whether that trust transfers to a small business with no existing audience and no name recognition. The AI-summary problem is real, and "they will click through for sources" is partially true but not in the volumes that used to drive blog traffic. We are testing this with our own challenge: zero existing readership, daily posts, see what happens.

3. Relatable, insuppressible content

Their take: "The unavoidable, tangible personality embedded within them is what sets blogs apart." Writers are imperfect; blogs expose that. Agendas, biases, fresh opinions. In a world of mundane AI platitudes, this still pops.

Where they are right: this is the strongest point in the article and the one we are betting our challenge on. AI slop has zero personality. Posts that argue with the reader have all of it. Yesterday's post about a Thai restaurant build came from a real frustration of mine, not from a content marketer running a "value-add post" through ChatGPT. That is the whole game.

Where it is worth pushing further: "real personality" is not free. It costs time. Most small businesses cannot write a thousand words of voice-driven content every day on top of running their actual business. Which is why we just spent today building a tool that writes in our voice for us; not generic AI, but our voice specifically. Yes, this very response was generated using it.

4. Constant evolution

Their take: blogs have evolved over 25 years from diaries to marketing tool. Still evolving. High-level bloggers earn millions. Influencer reviews from people who use the product daily beat marketing-department reviews.

Where they are right: the influencer review point is solid. A KitchenAid review from a chef who actually uses the mixer every day will out-earn the manufacturer's product page.

Where it is vague: "constant evolution" is the kind of thing you write when you do not know what the next phase is. Evolving how, for whom, in which markets, on what timescale? Our experiment is one specific evolution: a small business attempting to grow through daily organic content with zero ad spend. Other evolutions exist. Their article does not pick one, which is fine for a hosting-company piece, but tells you nothing about whether your evolution will work.

5. The infinite nature of niches

Their take: there is a blog out there on just about anything. Niche communities form around niche blogs. "If you can demonstrate passion, originality, and an untapped angle, an audience will follow."

Where they are right: this is the one we are literally testing right now. We are a niche audience: small business owners burned by marketing fluff and tired of agencies recommending whatever is hot this quarter. We bring passion, we bring originality, and we are shipping every day. By their thesis, an audience should follow.

Where the thesis gets tested in the real world: "an audience will follow" is a very confident sentence to write while the reader is sitting at their desk wondering if anyone will read what they are about to publish. Audiences do not follow because you have passion and originality. Audiences follow because the algorithm picks you up, or someone with reach shares you, or you happen to nail something searchable in week three. We will find out which of those happens, if any of them happens, in the next 20 days.

So, are blogs still relevant?

Their answer: a resounding yes.

Our answer: we do not know yet, and the fact that we are 10 days in and do not know is itself the answer for a lot of small businesses thinking about starting one. The Namecheap article is right about format strength, right about personality, right about niches. It is also written by a hosting company that wants you to buy hosting, and the only way to find out if their five reasons translate to your specific business in your specific market is to do the experiment.

We are doing the experiment. We will publish what we find on day 30, whether that is "yes, organic blogging works for a small business" or "the next 30-day challenge is 30 days of ad spend and a holiday" (half joking, half not).
