Today I Built a Quote Tool That Isn't a Form.

4 min read By PlainBlack 8 May 2026
← Back to Blog

Here's what most solar quote tools look like.

Name. Email. Phone. Address. Postcode. Power bill upload. "How big is your house?" dropdown. "What's your monthly electricity spend?" slider. Tick box if you have an EV. Tick box if you're considering a battery. Tick box for newsletter. Captcha. Submit.

Then nothing happens for two days. Then a sales call.

This is how 90% of solar companies do it. The customer leaves more annoyed than when they arrived, having coughed up six personal details for the privilege of being put in a queue.

Today I built a different one.

For Genr8 Energy. Tasmania. Solar, batteries, EV charging, the lot. Ian and I built their original site a few years back, then walked away when they didn't need us anymore. They're keen to come back. We want them back. So today's tool is a pitch piece, dressed up as a working demo.

It's at client.plainblackcreative.com/custom/genr8/solar-quote.

Type a Tasmania address. See your roof from space. Click around it.

That's the whole interaction model.

You don't fill in a form. You poke at a satellite photo of your own house. The tool draws an outline. Solar panels appear inside the outline as you close it off. A live meter under the photo ticks: 47 m² traced. 5.4 kW system. Saves $1,180 a year. Pays back in 5.6 years.

Watching numbers move while you do something feels different from watching a loading spinner after you submit something. The first one is play. The second one is admin.

The numbers are real.

Tasmania irradiance, 1300 kWh/m² a year. Aurora Energy peak rate, 28 cents per kWh. Aurora feed-in tariff, 8.9 cents. STC rebate baked into the install cost. Self-consumption tuned to a normal Tassie household.

We're not making numbers up. We're showing the actual maths and letting the buyer feel the shape of it before anyone has to talk on the phone.

If your roof is heavily shaded by trees, or the satellite imagery is older than your renovation, the tool doesn't pretend it can quote you. It routes you to a free site visit instead. "We'll drive by, takes about five minutes, you don't need to be home." That's outcome two.

Outcome three is "show me a range." For people who don't want to trace anything. The tool detects the suburb from the address and shows the typical kW band, the savings band, the payback band. Less precise, faster. Some buyers want sharp; some want a vibe.

Three exit doors. No dead ends. Whichever door you pick, the form at the bottom asks for the same three things: name, phone, email. That's it.

Why this matters for every service business.

Most service businesses give customers a wall of friction at the start. We trained ourselves to think of the quote form as a "lead magnet." It's the opposite. It's a lead repellent. Every field is a reason to bounce.

The trick isn't to remove the form. It's to replace the form with a thing the customer actually wants to play with, and then put one tiny form at the end, after they've already invested.

Roofers can do this. Painters. Landscapers. Pool installers. Electricians. Concreters. Cleaners. Lawn-mowing rounds. Anyone whose price scales with measurable physical stuff. You give them a satellite photo, you let them trace, you show them the maths in real time, you give them three exit doors.

The interesting part is that it doesn't change what you charge or how you do the work. It changes how the buyer feels about you before the first conversation. They show up already half-convinced.

What's actually live today.

The Genr8 demo runs in concept mode. Three Tasmania addresses are pre-loaded so the three outcomes can be demoed end-to-end without a Maps API key burning through credits. Sandy Bay drops you into the trace flow. Launceston into the suburb-range flow. Deloraine into the site-visit flow. Any other Tasmania address falls through to the suburb-range path so the demo never breaks.

When Genr8 say yes, we drop in a real Maps API key, wire the form to their lead pipeline, and reskin it with their actual brand assets instead of my best-guess green and gold.

I'd quite like that to be soon.

So if you're reading this and you know the Genr8 team, send them this link. Tell them Ian and Jay built their original site, and we've spent the time since learning to build the tools their site never had.

The pattern, named.

The hardest part of every solar buying decision is "is it actually worth it for my roof?" We just made that the first 60 seconds, instead of the last conversation.

The hardest part of every roofing job is "how big is the area?" Replace solar with roofing, the irradiance number with a paint-coverage rate, the panels with shingle counts. Same engine.

The hardest part of every paving job is "how many square metres of driveway?" Same engine again.

I'm starting to think this isn't one tool. It's a productised pattern that any quote-driven trade can buy off the shelf and have running in their brand by the end of the week.

That's what Day 8 actually shipped. A solar tool, yes. And the second proof that the pattern underneath is real.

Day 8 of 30. Eight tools out the door. The shape's getting clearer.
