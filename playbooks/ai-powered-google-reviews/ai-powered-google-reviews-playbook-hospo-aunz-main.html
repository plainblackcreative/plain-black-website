Why Reviews Are Your Most Valuable Marketing Channel
The numbers that will change how your venue thinks about every review.
Google Business Profile · Free
Pending
Goal: Understand the real commercial impact of Google reviews so your venue treats every one as the marketing asset it actually is.

Why this matters more than your next social post

  • 93% of diners read Google reviews before choosing a venue
  • A one-star increase in rating can lift revenue by 5 to 9%
  • Google reviews are the number one local SEO ranking signal
  • Review responses are read by 97% of people who read reviews
  • Venues that respond consistently earn 35% more revenue than those that don't

What most venues get wrong

  • Treating reviews as feedback instead of marketing
  • Only responding to negative reviews, ignoring positive ones
  • Generic, robotic responses that signal nobody's paying attention
  • Never asking happy regulars for a review
  • Missing the chance to rank higher in local "best cafe" or "best restaurant" searches
Common mistake: Your Google review profile is more visible than your Instagram page. Anyone searching for your type of venue in your suburb sees your rating before they see anything else. It costs nothing to improve it.
Step-by-step: Check your current review health ▼
1
Search your venue name on Google Maps. This is exactly what your customers see.
2
Note your star rating, total review count, and when your most recent review arrived.
3
Count how many of the last 20 reviews have a response. That's your current response rate.
4
Search your nearest competitor and compare their rating, review count, and response rate to yours.
5
Write down three numbers: your current rating, your response rate, and the gap to your nearest competitor. These are your baselines.

Setting Up Google Business Profile for Review Management
The settings most venues miss. Do this before you respond to anything.
Google Business Profile · Free
Pending
Goal: Get your Google Business Profile set up correctly so you never miss a review, the profile builds trust before anyone walks in, and every response works harder in local search.

Profile basics to check today

  • Business name matches exactly how customers search for you
  • Primary category is correct: "Coffee Shop," "Restaurant," "Bar," etc.
  • Hours are current, including public holiday variations for your state or region
  • Phone, website, and email are live and working
  • At least 10 photos: interior, food, drinks, team, and street view

Notification settings to turn on now

  • Email notifications to your main inbox for every new review
  • Push notifications via the Google Business Profile app on your phone
  • Weekly calendar reminder to check the dashboard
  • Add a backup manager so nothing gets missed on busy service days
Sanity check: Log into business.google.com right now and confirm notifications are on. A surprising number of venues have them off and only find out when a bad review has sat unanswered for weeks.
Step-by-step: Set up review notifications ▼
1
Go to business.google.com and sign in with the account that owns your profile.
2
Click the Settings icon in the left sidebar. Select Notifications.
3
Turn on all options under "Customer reviews and questions." Confirm emails go to your main business inbox.
4
Download the Google Business Profile app on your phone. Sign in and enable push notifications.
5
Go to Users and confirm the right people have access. Add a backup manager: front-of-house manager or business partner.

The Response Framework: Positive, Neutral, and Negative Reviews
A decision tree for every review type. Know what to do before you read a word.
ChatGPT · FreeNotion · Free
Pending
Goal: Build a decision framework so you always know exactly what to do with any review, before thinking about what to write.

The three review types

  • Positive (4-5 stars): amplify, thank personally, use their specific words
  • Neutral (3 stars): acknowledge specifically, invite back, address the gap
  • Negative (1-2 stars): own it without excuses, apologise, take it offline
  • No text, just stars: still respond. The public sees it.

Response time targets

  • Negative: within 24 hours. Every time, no exceptions.
  • Neutral: within 48 hours
  • Positive: within 72 hours. Sooner is always better.
  • Best time to check: right after your morning service when things quiet down
Common mistake: Writing a response to a negative review when you're still angry after a tough service. Draft it, save it, sleep on it, then send.
Step-by-step: Build your response decision tree ▼
1
Open a Notion page or Google Doc. Create three sections: Positive, Neutral, Negative.
2
Under each, write the first line you'll always use. Keep it human and specific to your venue's voice.
3
Add response time targets next to each type. Write them as rules, not goals.
4
Add a "draft and sleep" rule for any negative review that lands on a hard day.
5
Save this as your venue's Response Policy and share it with anyone who helps manage reviews.

04
Responding to Negative Reviews Without Making It Worse
Scripts, structure, and rules for the reviews that keep you up at night.
ChatGPT · Free
Pending
Goal: Build a word-for-word framework for every negative review so your response is calm, professional, and wins back the customer or impresses every future customer who reads it.

The four-part negative response structure

  • 1. Acknowledge specifically. Name the issue they raised.
  • 2. Apologise without excuses. No "but" or "however."
  • 3. Take it offline. Give a direct email or phone number.
  • 4. Keep it under 100 words. Brevity signals confidence.

What never to do

  • Never say "I'm sorry you felt that way." It's not an apology.
  • Never argue, explain, or justify in the response
  • Never mention other customers or compare experiences
  • Never respond on a bad day. Draft, sleep, send.
  • Never ask them to remove the review in your public response
Remember: Your response to a negative review is not for the person who wrote it. It's for every future customer who reads it. Write for them.
Step-by-step: Respond to a negative review right now ▼
1
Find the most recent negative review on your Google profile.
2
Identify the specific complaint: wait time, food quality, service, noise, price, etc.
3
Open ChatGPT. Use the prompt from Section 06 to draft a response. Paste in the review text.
4
Edit the draft to sound like your venue. Cut anything defensive or over-apologetic.
5
Add your direct email or phone number with a specific invitation to follow up.
6
Read it aloud. If it sounds like a press release, rewrite it. If it sounds like a real person wrote it, post it.

The AI Response System: Prompts That Sound Like Your Venue
The exact ChatGPT prompts that generate on-brand responses in under 60 seconds.
ChatGPT · Free
Pending
Goal: Set up a ChatGPT prompt system that generates review responses in your venue's voice, not generic AI voice, so every response sounds like it came from the person behind the counter.

The master prompt structure

  • Start with: "You are responding to Google reviews on behalf of [Venue Name], a [venue type] in [suburb, city]."
  • Describe the brand voice: warm, direct, neighbourhood feel, no corporate language
  • State the review type: positive, neutral, or negative
  • Paste the review text in quotes
  • Ask for a response under 100 words with no filler phrases

Copy-paste response templates

  • 5-star: "Thank you [name], this genuinely made our week. We'll see you soon."
  • 3-star: "Thanks for the honest feedback, [name]. We'd love to make it up to you. Reach out anytime."
  • 1-star: "That's not the experience we want for anyone. Please reach out to us directly at [email] and we'll sort it."
  • No text: "Thanks for the stars, [name]. Hope to see you again soon."
Sanity check: Always edit the AI output before posting. ChatGPT is your first draft, not your finished response. If you can't tell the difference between the AI version and something you'd actually write, it's ready to post.
Step-by-step: Set up your venue's ChatGPT prompt ▼
1
Open ChatGPT at chat.openai.com. Free tier works fine.
2
Start a new chat. Paste this context: "You are responding to Google reviews on behalf of [Your Venue Name], a [cafe/restaurant/bar] in [suburb, city]. The brand voice is warm, direct, and human. No corporate language, no exclamation marks on every sentence, no 'valued customer.' Responses should be under 100 words and sound like the owner wrote them."
3
Then add: "Here is a [positive/negative/neutral] review. Please draft a response: [paste review text]"
4
Read the output aloud. Edit anything that doesn't sound like your venue.
5
Save this prompt template in Notion or a Google Doc so it's ready every time.

Getting More 5-Star Reviews Without Begging
Timing, phrasing, and a QR system that makes asking feel natural.
Google Business Profile · FreeQR Code Generator · Free
Pending
Goal: Build a simple, repeatable system that consistently generates more genuine 5-star reviews, without asking in a way that feels desperate or kills the vibe.

When to ask (timing is everything)

  • Right after a compliment at the counter or table
  • When a regular picks up their order and there's a natural moment
  • Via a QR code tent card on tables or at the counter
  • Never during a rush or when the customer is clearly in a hurry
  • Never right after a complaint or a long wait

What to say (word for word)

  • "If you enjoyed it, a Google review genuinely helps us out. Scan the QR on the table."
  • One line. Casual. No pressure.
  • Train every team member to use the same line so it becomes natural
  • The QR code does the heavy lifting. You just need to mention it.
Google policy: You cannot offer incentives for reviews. A free coffee for a 5-star review can get your reviews removed and your profile flagged. Ask genuinely. It's also more effective.
Step-by-step: Create and deploy your QR code ▼
1
Go to business.google.com. Find the "Get more reviews" section on your dashboard.
2
Copy the short review link Google provides for your venue.
3
Go to qr-code-generator.com (free). Paste the link and generate a QR code. Download it.
4
Print and place on tables, at the counter, and on takeaway packaging. Add a label: "Enjoyed your visit? Leave us a Google review."
5
Test it by scanning with your own phone first. Confirm it goes directly to the review screen.

08
Handling Fake, Malicious, and Competitor Reviews
How to flag, report, and respond to reviews you know aren't legitimate.
Google Business Profile · Free
Pending
Goal: Give you a clear process for identifying, reporting, and responding to fake or malicious reviews, and understanding what Google will and won't remove.

Signs a review may not be legitimate

  • Reviewer has no photo and zero other reviews
  • Review references things that never happened at your venue
  • Language doesn't match how real customers talk about your type of venue
  • Multiple 1-star reviews arrive within days with no specific complaints
  • Reviewer account was created very recently

What Google will remove

  • Reviews that violate Google's content policy (spam, hate speech, off-topic)
  • Reviews from someone who never visited your venue
  • Coordinated fake review attacks (requires documentation)
  • Reviews that include personal information or threats
  • Google does NOT remove reviews just because they are negative
Important: Do not respond aggressively to a review you believe is fake. Respond calmly: "We don't have any record of a visit matching this description. Please reach out to us directly so we can look into it."
Step-by-step: Flag a suspicious review on Google ▼
1
Go to your Google Maps listing and find the review in question.
2
Click the three dots (...) next to the review. Select "Report review."
3
Choose the most accurate reason: spam, off-topic, conflict of interest, or policy violation.
4
If not removed within a week, go to business.google.com and use the "Request review removal" tool under Reviews.
5
If still not resolved, escalate to the Google Business Profile Help Community with evidence: screenshots, timestamps, and any records that contradict the review.

The 15-Minute Weekly Review Routine
A repeatable process that keeps your profile healthy, even on the busiest weeks.
Notion · FreeGoogle Calendar · Free
Pending
Goal: Build a 15-minute weekly routine that keeps your review profile healthy, responsive, and growing, every week, without relying on memory or good intentions.

The 15-minute Monday routine

  • Minutes 1 to 3: Check notifications. Note any new reviews from the past week.
  • Minutes 4 to 8: Respond to all unanswered reviews using the ChatGPT prompts from Section 06.
  • Minutes 9 to 11: Flag the best new positive review for content use (Section 05).
  • Minutes 12 to 14: Check your current rating and response rate in the dashboard.
  • Minute 15: Update this week's row in the tracking sheet (Section 10).

How to make sure it actually happens

  • Set a recurring Monday event in your calendar: "Reviews, 15 min" right after morning service
  • Keep this playbook open in a browser tab
  • If you miss a week: don't skip two. Respond to everything and restart.
  • Tell one team member about it so there's someone to keep you accountable
Accountability tip: The best time for this routine is right after the Monday morning service, when the venue is quiet and you're still in work mode. Block it at the same time every week and don't move it.
Step-by-step: Set up your weekly routine right now ▼
1
Open Google Calendar or whatever calendar you actually use.
2
Create a recurring event: every Monday, 15 minutes, title "Reviews." Set it for right after the morning service rush.
3
In the event description, paste the 5-step routine from the left column above.
4
Add this playbook URL to the event description so you always start here.
5
Run the routine now. Don't wait until Monday. Respond to everything currently unanswered on your profile.

Tracking, Reporting, and What Good Looks Like
The simple sheet that tells you whether the system is working.
Google Sheets · FreeGoogle Business Profile · Free
Pending
Goal: Set up a simple Google Sheet that lets you track your review health over time so you can see what's working, catch problems early, and have real data to show growth.

What to track weekly (takes 2 minutes)

  • Date of weekly check
  • Current star rating
  • Total review count
  • New reviews this week
  • Reviews responded to this week
  • Response rate % this week

What good looks like

  • Response rate above 90% every week
  • Star rating trend stable or increasing over 90 days
  • At least 2 to 4 new reviews per month
  • No negative review left unanswered for more than 24 hours
  • Rating trend clearly improving versus your baseline from Section 01
When to investigate: If your rating drops 0.2 points or more in a month, check for a pattern. Are the new negatives all about the same thing? Slow service, a particular team member, pricing? That's operational feedback worth acting on, not just a response to manage.
Step-by-step: Set up your review tracking sheet ▼
1
Go to sheets.google.com. Create a new spreadsheet. Title it "[Your Venue] Review Tracker".
2
Create these column headers: Date, Star Rating, Total Reviews, New This Week, Responded, Response Rate %, Notes.
3
Fill in today's row as your baseline. Check current stats at business.google.com.
4
Update the sheet every Monday as part of the routine from Section 09.
5
After 8 weeks, add a simple line chart of your star rating over time. Seeing the line go up is the proof the system works.
